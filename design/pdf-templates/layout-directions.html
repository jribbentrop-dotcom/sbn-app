The Major Seventh Chord with 9
Formula
Root · 3 · 5 · 7 · 9 — the major triad with a major seventh and a major ninth on top.
Sound
The ninth adds extra warmth to the major triad with a major seventh — open, lush, unmistakably bossa.
Where you'll hear it
The first chord of The Girl from Ipanema — and the opening chord of countless bossa nova standards since.
Cross-reference

The Minor Seventh Chord with 9

The little sister of the Major9 chord — a bit more mellow, and at least as beautiful.

Drop the third of a plain m7 chord by a step and you land on the m9 — softer, rounder, and far less stark than its parent chord. Where the m7 can feel a little severe in isolation, the added ninth rounds out the voicing and gives it room to breathe, which is exactly why it shows up everywhere in jazz-leaning bossa nova writing.

The jazz bossa standard Blue Bossa makes extensive use of this chord — try comping through the changes slowly before bringing it up to tempo, and listen for how the ninth colours the line differently depending on what the melody is doing above it.
Suggested Practice Pattern
Listen
Blue Bossa — Kenny Dorham / Joe Henderson. Note how the m9 sits under the melody in the A section.
Try this
Play the pattern with your thumb only, then add fingerstyle arpeggiation once the shape feels secure.
Related
Compare with the plain m7 shell voicing (Shell Voicings booklet, p.1) — same family, different colour.
