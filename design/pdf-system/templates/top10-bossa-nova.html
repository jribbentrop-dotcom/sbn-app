Chord Theory in a Nutshell

Bossa Nova is famous for its soft, warm-sounding chords. Most of them are four-part chords — chords built from four notes instead of the three you’d find in a basic major or minor triad. Those four notes have names: the root, the 3rd, the 5th, and the 7th. Stack them up and you get a seventh chord — the basic building block behind almost everything in this catalog.

On top of that four-note core, you can add an extension tone for extra color: a 9th, an 11th, or a 13th. The 9th is the most common in Bossa Nova — it’s what gives chords like the Major 6/9 (Item #1) or the Minor 7(9) (Item #2) their warmth. You’ll see extensions written in parentheses after the chord name, like Cm7(9) — that just means “add the 9th on top.”

Guitar chords aren’t usually played as a plain stack of those four (or five) notes in order — the notes get rearranged across the strings for a shape that’s actually playable. This catalog groups those rearrangements into a few families you’ll see named throughout: a Shell voicing keeps just the root, 3rd, and 7th (plus the extension), for a lean, open sound. A Drop 2 or Drop 3 voicing takes a normal stacked chord and drops one note down an octave into the bass, spreading the chord across more strings for a fuller sound. A few chords in this set use custom voicings that don’t fit either pattern — built by ear, for a specific color.

Each entry in this catalog gives you the chord’s diagram, a short Voicing note explaining how that particular shape is built, and a Voicing caption with the musical or historical context behind it — those two are kept deliberately separate, so you get both the mechanics and the music. Underneath, a Suggested Practice Pattern shows the chord in a real progression (most are pulled directly from actual recordings), paired with a named rhythm pattern. In the margin, three short asides round out each entry: Listen points you to the song it’s drawn from, Try this gives you a related exercise or progression to extend the idea, and Related points to where the full progression is taught in depth.

With the theory out of the way — here are ten of the most useful, recognizable chords in the Bossa Nova vocabulary.

01
The Major 6/9 Chord
D♭6(9)/A♭

The opening sound of Bossa Nova — the chord that introduced a genre to the world.

This is the chord — the opening voicing of The Girl from Ipanema on the Getz/Gilberto recording, and the sound that introduced Bossa Nova to the world. Despite its complex name (Major 6/9 with the 5th in the bass), it’s a relaxed, open shape that sits naturally under the fingers.

This Major 6/9 is a custom voicing rather than a standard chord shape: instead of the usual stack of 3rds, the notes here are arranged in 4ths. That’s what gives the chord its open, slightly suspended sound compared to a conventional Major 6/9 grip. João Gilberto loved to play the 5th in the bass — this is the quintessential Bossa Nova chord, as heard at the very beginning of The Girl from Ipanema on the famous Getz/Gilberto recording.

D♭6(9)/A♭
Custom

02
Minor Seventh with 9
Cm7(9)

The little sister of the Major 6/9 — a bit more mellow, just as beautiful.

The minor 7th with a 9th on top softens the chord’s dark quality and gives it an airy, floating feel that’s essential for minor-key Bossa Nova. Kenny Dorham’s Blue Bossa is built almost entirely around this sound.

This is a Shell voicing rooted on the A-string — just the root, 3rd, and 7th, with the 9th added on top for color. It’s compact and transparent, and the open lower strings leave the thumb free to walk a bassline underneath. Drop this shape onto the first four bars of Blue Bossa and you’ll hear immediately why it’s a jam-session staple — the entire A section barely strays from this one voicing.

03
The Minor Sixth Chord
Am6

Where the m7 floats, this one resolves — the grounded, final-sounding tonic of Jobim ballads.

The minor 6th chord replaces the 7th with a major 6th — a subtle swap that changes the mood entirely. Where the m7 feels unresolved and floating, the m6 feels grounded and final. That quality of stillness-within-minor is the defining color of Jobim ballads like Corcovado and Insensatez.

This is a Drop 3 voicing — one of the four chord tones is dropped down an octave into the bass while the rest stack on top. Compared to a Shell voicing, it spreads the chord across more strings, trading one extra finger for a fuller, more resonant sound. Let the low root ring out and keep the upper voices quiet and clean.

Dominant Seventh with 13
G7(13)

A complicated name for a simple, elegant sound — the dominant chord of warm, sun-drenched Bossa Nova resolutions.

The 13th sits just a major 6th above the root — adding brightness and color without extra tension. It’s the dominant chord Jobim used for smooth, sun-drenched resolutions in Wave, The Girl from Ipanema, and dozens of other songs.

Another Drop 3 voicing, built the same way as the Minor Sixth shape in Item #3. The voicing puts the 13th as the highest note — that bright top voice is what you hear lifting above the bass in Wave’s intro. Keep the touch light so the extension sings.

05
The Half Diminished Chord
Bm7♭5

The II chord in every minor jazz cadence — unsettled, questioning, inevitable.

The half-diminished — also written m7♭5 or ø — is the II chord in every minor jazz cadence. It has an unsettled, questioning quality that makes the dominant that follows feel inevitable. Learn this shape once and you’ll recognize it in Manhã de Carnaval, Chega de Saudade, Blue Bossa, and virtually every minor-key standard in the repertoire.

This is a Drop 2 voicing rooted on the A-string — one chord tone is dropped down an octave from a Shell-style stack, spreading the chord across four strings for a fuller sound. This is one of the most versatile jazz and bossa nova voicings in more ways than one (see “Try this”). The shape is fully moveable — slide it to any fret and you have a new root.

Bm7♭5
Drop 2
Movable shape — root on the 5th string. Position 2.

06
Dominant Seventh with 9
D7(9)

One of the most practical chords in the guitar repertoire — lean, warm, and perfectly placed.

The Dom7(9) shell voicing is one of the most practical chords in the guitar repertoire. The 9th adds warmth without complexity, and the open shell structure leaves space for a bassline in the thumb. Listen to Astrud Gilberto’s recording of Jobim’s Fotografia: the intro builds a hypnotic cross rhythm, and the D7(9) arrives at the end of the phrase like a question mark — tense, bright, and perfectly placed.

This is a Shell voicing rooted on the A-string — just the root, major 3rd, and minor 7th, with the 9th stacked on top for color. Same shape family as Item #2’s Cm7(9), just dominant quality instead of minor. This is exactly how João Gilberto comped: minimal, spacious, in the pocket.

D7(9)

Dominant Seventh with ♭9
E7(♭9)

The most tension-laden chord in minor jazz harmony — the ♭9 aches to resolve.

The V7(♭9) is the most tension-laden chord in minor jazz harmony. The ♭9 sits just a half-step above the root, creating a dissonance that aches to resolve. The Gentle Rain gives you the ultimate workout: a chain of minor ii–V’s, each Dom7(♭9) tightening the tension before the next ii takes over. An outstanding training progression.

Same Shell-voicing family as Item #6’s D7(9) — just with a ♭9 instead of a 9 on top. The shell voicing keeps it lean: root, major 3rd, minor 7th, and ♭9. Let the ♭9 ring, then feel the relief when it resolves down a half-step to the minor tonic.

E7(♭9)
Shell
Movable shape — root on the 5th string.

The Diminished Seventh Chord
C♯dim7

Jobim’s secret weapon — perfectly symmetrical, one shape gives you four chords at once.

The diminished seventh is built entirely from minor thirds stacked on top of each other — which makes it perfectly symmetrical. Every note in the chord can function as the root, meaning one shape gives you four chords at once. Jobim exploited this in How Insensitive, Desafinado, and especially Once I Loved, where diminished passing chords slide in half-steps to create smooth chromatic motion between any two chords.

A Drop 2 voicing of the diminished 7th — four notes, all a minor 3rd apart, which is what makes the whole shape symmetrical. Try sliding this shape up three frets: you get the same chord with a different inversion. C♯°7, E°7, G°7, and B♭°7 are all the same four notes.

C♯dim7
Drop 2
Symmetrical shape — slides up 3 frets = same chord.
Listen
Desafinado — a passing diminished bridges the chords in this exact spot, bars 36–39.
Try this
This shape gives you four chords at once: C♯°7, E°7, G°7, B♭°7. Resolve each a half-step up into a different tonic for four completely different contexts.

09
Diminished with Flat 13
G♯dim7(♭13)

Item #8 with one note added — that single extra finger turns a sharp passing chord into one that floats.

A refinement of the diminished seventh — adding the ♭13 softens the chord’s harsh edge and gives it a more ambiguous, modal quality. Where the plain °7 cuts sharply, the °7(♭13) blurs the outline just enough to feel more impressionistic. This was a characteristic touch of João Gilberto, who used it in Corcovado to keep the diminished passing chord from sounding too abrupt against the song’s quiet atmosphere.

The same symmetrical diminished shape as Item #8, with one note added: a ♭13 stacked on top. The drop-3 voicing places the root on the low E string for a full, resonant sound. The ♭13 is the added color note — one extra finger, but it changes the emotional quality of the chord completely.

G♯dim7(♭13)
Drop 3
Root on E string. The ♭13 is the added color finger.

Dominant Seventh with ♭13
G7(♭13)

One semitone from Item #4 — that single half-step flips warm and optimistic into dark and serious.

Compare this to Item #4: both are dominant sevenths with a 13th, but the ♭13 sits a half-step lower — and that single semitone changes everything. Where the Dom7(13) sounds warm and optimistic, the Dom7(♭13) sounds darker, more serious, pulling toward minor rather than major. Bossa Nova and jazz players like to play with this expectation and resolve it to a major tonic for a surprising twist.

The same Drop 3 shape as Item #4’s G7(13), with the 13th flatted by a half-step — same fingering family, one fret lower on that top note. Notice the tension: the top note wants to resolve downward by a half-step to the 9th of the target chord.

G7(♭13)
Drop 3
Movable shape — root on the 6th string. Position 2.
Listen
S’Wonderful — Gilberto uses this exact color to turn the B-part’s modulation into a Minor Blues Cadence.

Compare directly with Item #4: play G7(13), then G7(♭13) — same shape, one fret lower on the top note. Same chord, two completely different emotional colors.
