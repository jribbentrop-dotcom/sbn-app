### Chord Theory in a Nutshell

*(Adapted from the source PDF's intro page, rewritten to match this catalog's own voicing vocabulary —
Shell / Drop 2 / Drop 3 / custom — instead of the old generic diagram-and-TAB legend, since that vocabulary
now carries the whole booklet.)*

> Bossa Nova is famous for its soft, warm-sounding chords. Most of them are four-part chords — chords built
> from four notes instead of the three you'd find in a basic major or minor triad. Those four notes have
> names: the root, the 3rd, the 5th, and the 7th. Stack them up and you get a seventh chord — the basic
> building block behind almost everything in this catalog.
>
> On top of that four-note core, you can add an extension tone for extra color: a 9th, an 11th, or a 13th.
> The 9th is the most common in Bossa Nova — it's what gives chords like the Major 6/9 (Item #1) or the
> Minor 7(9) (Item #2) their warmth. You'll see extensions written in parentheses after the chord name, like
> Cm7(9) — that just means "add the 9th on top."
>
> Guitar chords aren't usually played as a plain stack of those four (or five) notes in order — the notes
> get rearranged across the strings for a shape that's actually playable. This catalog groups those
> rearrangements into a few families you'll see named throughout: a **Shell** voicing keeps just the root,
> 3rd, and 7th (plus the extension), for a lean, open sound. A **Drop 2** or **Drop 3** voicing takes a
> normal stacked chord and drops one note down an octave into the bass, spreading the chord across more
> strings for a fuller sound. A few chords in this set use **custom** voicings that don't fit either
> pattern — built by ear, for a specific color.
>
> Each entry in this catalog gives you the chord's diagram, a short **Voicing note** explaining how that
> particular shape is built, and a **Voicing caption** with the musical or historical context behind it —
> those two are kept deliberately separate, so you get both the mechanics and the music. Underneath, a
> **Suggested Practice Pattern** shows the chord in a real progression (most are pulled directly from actual
> recordings), paired with a named rhythm pattern. In the margin, three short asides round out each entry:
> **Listen** points you to the song it's drawn from, **Try this** gives you a related exercise or
> progression to extend the idea, and **Related** points to where the full progression is taught in depth.
>
> With the theory out of the way — here are ten of the most useful, recognizable chords in the Bossa Nova
> vocabulary.

## Item #2 — Minor Seventh with 9

**Citation:** Kenny Dorham, *Page One*, 1963 (the A section of *Blue Bossa*)

**Catalog display root: C** — shown as **Cm7(9)** — matches the practice pattern exactly, no key
adjustment needed here.

### Body copy (adapted from website)

> The little sister of the Major 6/9 — a bit more mellow, just as beautiful. The minor 7th with a 9th on
> top softens the chord's dark quality and gives it an airy, floating feel that's essential for minor-key
> Bossa Nova. Kenny Dorham's *Blue Bossa* is built almost entirely around this sound.

### Voicing note

> This is a Shell voicing rooted on the A-string — just the root, 3rd, and 7th, with the 9th added on top
> for color. It's compact and transparent, and the open lower strings leave the thumb free to walk a
> bassline underneath.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> jam-session staple — the entire A section barely strays from this one voicing.

## Item #3 — The Minor Sixth Chord

**Citation:** João Gilberto, *O Amor, O Sorriso E A Flor*, 1960 (opening chord of *Corcovado*)

**Catalog display root: A** — shown as **Am6**, matches the website exactly.

### Body copy (from website)

> The minor 6th chord replaces the 7th with a major 6th — a subtle swap that changes the mood entirely.
> Where the m7 feels unresolved and floating, the m6 feels grounded and final. That quality of
> stillness-within-minor is the defining color of Jobim ballads like *Corcovado* and *Insensatez*.

### Voicing note

> This is a Drop 3 voicing — one of the four chord tones is dropped down an octave into the bass while
> the rest stack on top. Compared to a Shell voicing, it spreads the chord across more strings, trading
> one extra finger for a fuller, more resonant sound.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> Where the m7 floats, this one resolves — it's the grounded, final-sounding tonic João Gilberto reached
> for to open *Corcovado*. Let the low root ring out and keep the upper voices quiet and clean.

*(Per the old PDF: "The minor 6th chord is the darker alternative to the minor 7th chord. It is almost
always suitable as a tonic chord in minor pieces such as Corcovado." — folded the "darker/grounded vs.
floating m7" contrast in above so the note and caption no longer overlap.)*

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: DB leadsheet `top10`, bars 9–12 (Am6 → Dm7(9)). This also matches the old PDF's own suggested
pattern for this item ("Am – Dm()"), so it's confirmed from both sources.

| Bar | Chord | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1–2 | Am6 | home position — the tonic |
| 3–4 | Dm7(9) | same shape, moved up a 4th |

**Rhythm: Extended Gilberto Rhythm · 2/4 · sixteenth-note grid** (no bpm shown, per your note).

> A variation of the basic Gilberto Rhythm — pulling the first-beat accent a sixteenth note earlier
> gives it more forward drive and momentum than its plainer sister.

### Margin asides

- **Listen:** *Corcovado* — this chord opens the song, Example #7 in this booklet.
- **Try this:** Move the whole Am6 → Abo7(b13) pairing around the cycle of fourths — Dm6 → Dbo7(b13), Gm6 → Gbo7(b13), and on. Same tonic-and-passing-chord relationship, any minor key.
- **Related:** The Corcovado Progression built around this chord is taught in full in the *Gilberto Plays Jobim* course.

## Item #4 — Dominant Seventh with 13

**Citation:** Antonio Carlos Jobim, *Wave*, 1967 (the intro's ii–V)

**Catalog display root: G** — shown as **G7(13)**, matches the website exactly.

### Body copy (from website)

> A complicated name for a simple, elegant sound. The 13th sits just a major 6th above the root — adding
> brightness and color without extra tension. It's the dominant chord Jobim used for smooth,
> sun-drenched resolutions in *Wave*, *The Girl from Ipanema*, and dozens of other songs.

### Voicing note

> Another Drop 3 voicing, built the same way as the Minor Sixth shape in Item #3 — one chord tone drops
> out of the usual stack (here the 5th, left out entirely) and the rest spread across four strings with
> two left muted in between.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> The voicing puts the 13th as the highest note — that bright top voice is what you hear lifting above
> the bass in *Wave*'s intro. Keep the touch light so the extension sings.

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: DB leadsheet `top10`, bars 20–21 (Dm7(9) → G7(13)) — the ii–V into this chord, not a 4th-shift
drill this time. This matches both the website's named **Jazz Half Cadence** progression (seed key C:
"G7(13) resolving to CMaj7 is one of the most characteristic sounds in Bossa Nova") and the real *Wave*
intro itself, bars 0–1, which is the exact same Dm7(9) → G7(13) pair.

| Bar | Chord | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Dm7(9) | the ii — sets up the cadence |
| 2 | G7(13) | home position — resolves outward to CMaj7 |

**Rhythm: Jazz Bossa Nova · 4/4 · eighth-note grid** (no bpm shown, per your note).

> The #1 go-to rhythm for giving any bossa song a jazz treatment — use it anywhere.

### Margin asides

- **Listen:** *Wave* — this exact ii–V opens the song; the same chord also appears in Example #4 (*Manhã de Carnaval*).
- **Try this:** Resolve the cadence all the way: Dm7(9) → G7(13) → Cmaj7. Then move the whole three-chord cell around the cycle of fourths — Gm7(9) → C7(13) → Fmaj7, and on.
- **Related:** The Jazz Half Cadence built around this chord is taught in full in the *Music Theory Basics* course.

## Item #5 — The Half Diminished Chord

**Citation:** Luiz Bonfá, *Solo In Rio*, 1959 (the central ii–V of *Manhã de Carnaval*)

**Catalog display root: B** — shown as **Bm7b5**, matches the website exactly.

### Body copy (from website)

> The half-diminished — also written m7♭5 or ø — is the II chord in every minor jazz cadence. It has an
> unsettled, questioning quality that makes the dominant that follows feel inevitable. Learn this shape
> once and you'll recognize it in *Manhã de Carnaval*, *Chega de Saudade*, *Blue Bossa*, and virtually
> every minor-key standard in the repertoire.

### Voicing note

> This is a Drop 2 voicing rooted on the A-string — one chord tone is dropped down an octave from a
> Shell-style stack, spreading the chord across four strings for a fuller sound than a Shell voicing
> alone.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> This is one of the most versatile jazz and bossa nova voicings — versatile in more ways than one, as
> it turns out (see "Try this"). The shape is fully moveable — slide it to any fret and you have a new
> root.

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: DB leadsheet `top10`, bars 15–16 (Bm7b5 → E7, resolving to Am6) — the minor ii–V–i in its
purest form, matching the website's named **Minor Jazz Cadence** progression exactly ("leans forward
into the dominant and won't feel resolved until it reaches the Am tonic"). The landing chord is this
catalog's own Item #3 tonic.

| Bar | Chord | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Bm7b5 → E7 | the ii–V — unsettled, leaning forward |
| 2 | Am6 | the i — resolves into Item #3's tonic |

**Rhythm: Choro · 2/4 · sixteenth-note grid** (DB slug `bonfa` — no bpm shown, per your note).

> Luiz Bonfá's syncopated guitar comping, in the classic Brazilian Choro style — this exact pattern is
> from his most famous composition, *Manhã de Carnaval*.

### Margin asides

- **Listen:** *Manhã de Carnaval* — this exact ii–V, Example #4 in this booklet.
- **Try this:** Move the whole ii–V–i around the cycle of fourths — Em7b5 → A7 → Dm, then Am7b5 → D7 → Gm, and on. Same suspended-to-resolved feeling, any minor key. Advanced: this same shape is a true chameleon — the identical notes also spell G7(9)/B or Dm(6)/B. Depending on the bass note underneath, one fingering can function as a half-diminished ii, a rootless dominant 9, or a minor 6 tonic.
- **Related:** The Minor Jazz Cadence built around this chord is taught in full in the *Music Theory Basics* course.

## Item #6 — Dominant Seventh with 9

**Citation:** Astrud Gilberto, *Fotografia*, 1965 (the chord that closes the intro phrase)

**Catalog display root: D** — shown as **D7(9)**, matches the website exactly.

### Body copy (from website)

> The Dom7(9) shell voicing is one of the most practical chords in the guitar repertoire. The 9th adds
> warmth without complexity, and the open shell structure leaves space for a bassline in the thumb.
> Listen to Astrud Gilberto's recording of Jobim's *Fotografia*: the intro builds a hypnotic cross
> rhythm, and the D7(9) arrives at the end of the phrase like a question mark — tense, bright, and
> perfectly placed.

### Voicing note

> This is a Shell voicing rooted on the A-string — just the root, 3rd, and 7th, with the 9th stacked on
> top for color. Same shape family as Item #2's Cm7(9), just dominant quality instead of minor.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> A shell voicing uses only the essential chord tones — root, major 3rd, minor 7th, and 9th. This is
> exactly how João Gilberto comped: minimal, spacious, in the pocket.

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: the *Fotografia* leadsheet itself, bars 0–3 — the song's actual intro, and a cleaner
illustration of the **Modal Interchange I to Im** progression than anything in the generic `top10`
sequence: I major → Im (borrowed minor) → the D7(9) pivot, which in the full intro resolves back to
Amaj7 right after.

| Bar | Chord | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1–2 | Amaj7 | the major tonic (I) |
| 3 | Am7 | borrowed from the parallel minor (Im) |
| 4 | D7(9) | home position — the bright, modal-interchange dominant, resolving back to Amaj7 just after |

**Rhythm: Cross Rhythm · 4/4 · sixteenth-note grid** (DB slug `cross-rhythm-i` — no bpm shown).

> The intro builds a hypnotic cross rhythm — a syncopated push-pull between thumb and fingers that sets
> up the dominant's late arrival like a question mark.

### Margin asides

- **Listen:** *Fotografia* — this exact 4-bar intro; the D7(9) lands right at the end of the phrase.
- **Try this:** Use only this voicing to play a full 12-bar blues — same Dom7(9) shape, just sliding it to the I, IV, and V chords. One shape, three positions, an entire blues.
- **Related:** The Modal Interchange I to Im progression built around this chord is taught in full in the *Music Theory Basics* course.

> The shell voicing keeps it lean: root, major 3rd, minor 7th, and ♭9. The ♭9 on top is the dissonant

## Item #8 — The Diminished Seventh Chord

**Citation:** Stan Getz & João Gilberto, *Getz/Gilberto*, 1964 (the passing diminished in *Desafinado*, bars 36–39)

**Catalog display root: C#** — shown as **C#dim7**, matches the website exactly.

### Body copy (from website)

> Jobim's secret weapon. The diminished seventh is built entirely from minor thirds stacked on top of
> each other — which makes it perfectly symmetrical. Every note in the chord can function as the root,
> meaning one shape gives you four chords at once. Jobim exploited this in *How Insensitive*,
> *Desafinado*, and especially *Once I Loved*, where diminished passing chords appear throughout —
> sliding the shape up or down in half-steps to create smooth chromatic motion between any two chords.

### Voicing note

> A Drop 2 voicing of the diminished 7th — four notes, all a minor 3rd apart, which is what makes the
> whole shape symmetrical.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> The diminished chord's special trick on guitar: try sliding this shape up three frets — you get the
> same chord with a different inversion. That's the symmetry at work: C#°7, E°7, G°7, and B♭°7 are all
> the same four notes.

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: DB leadsheet `top10`, bars 18–20 (Cmaj7 → C#dim7 → Dm7(9)) — matches the website's named **The
Ascending Diminished** progression exactly: the °7 acts as a chromatic bridge between the I and the
II7, the same motion Jobim used in *Once I Loved* and the verse of *Desafinado*. This also overlaps
directly with Item #4's bars (Dm7(9) → G7(13) follows right after) — the leadsheet runs these three
items as one continuous phrase.

| Bar | Chord | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cmaj7 | the I |
| 2 | C#dim7 | home position — chromatic bridge, half-step up from C |
| 3 | Dm7(9) | the II7 it resolves into |

**Rhythm: Gilberto Rhythm · 2/4 · sixteenth-note grid** (no per-item override found yet — flag if you
want something else here).

> João Gilberto's signature rhythm — a steady bass pulse from the thumb under off-beat, syncopated chord
> plucks from the fingers, distilled from the more complex patterns of samba.

### Margin asides

- **Listen:** *Desafinado* — a passing diminished bridges the chords in this exact spot, bars 36–39.
- **Try this:** Because of the symmetry, this one shape already gives you four diminished chords — C#°7, E°7, G°7, B♭°7. Try resolving each a half-step up into a different major or minor chord, and hear four completely different passing-chord contexts from one fingering.
- **Related:** The Ascending Diminished progression built around this chord is taught in full in the *Music Theory Basics* course.

## Item #10 — Dominant Seventh with b13

**Citation:** João Gilberto, *S'Wonderful*, 1978 (the B-part modulation, bars 14–17)

**Catalog display root: G** — shown as **G7(b13)**, matches the website exactly.

### Body copy (from website)

> Compare this to Item #4: both are dominant sevenths with a 13th, but the ♭13 sits a half-step lower —
> and that single semitone changes everything. Where the Dom7(13) sounds warm and optimistic, the
> Dom7(♭13) sounds darker, more serious, pulling toward minor rather than major. Bossa Nova and jazz
> players like to play with this expectation and resolve it to a major tonic for a surprising twist.

### Voicing note

> The same Drop 3 shape as Item #4's G7(13), with the 13th flatted by a half-step — same fingering
> family, one fret lower on that top note.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> The drop-3 voicing mirrors chord #4 in shape, but the ♭13 on top gives it a completely different
> color. Notice the tension: that top note wants to resolve downward by a half-step to the 9th of the
> target chord.

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: DB leadsheet `top10`, bars 60–62 (Dm7b5 → G7(b13) → Cm7(9)) — a full minor ii–V–i, matching the
website's named **Minor Blues Cadence** (seed key Cm) exactly, and closing the loop by resolving into
Item #2's tonic.

Each example pulls real chord data straight from its DB leadsheet (bar numbers below match the leadsheet's

as a "family match" rather than an exact one.
