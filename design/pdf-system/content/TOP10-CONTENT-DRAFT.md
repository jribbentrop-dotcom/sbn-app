### Chord Theory in a Nutshell

*(Adapted from the source PDF's intro page, rewritten to match this catalog's own voicing vocabulary —
Shell / Drop 2 / Drop 3 / custom — instead of the old generic diagram-and-TAB legend, since that vocabulary
now carries the whole booklet.)*

> Bossa Nova is famous for its soft, warm-sounding chords. Most of them are four-part chords — chords built
> from four notes instead of the three you'd find in a basic major or minor triad. Those four notes have
> names: the root, the 3rd, the 5th, and the 7th. Stack them up and you get a seventh chord — the basic
> building block behind almost everything in this catalog.
>
> On top of that four-note core, you can add an extension tone for extra color: a 9th, an 11th, or a 13th.
> The 9th is the most common in Bossa Nova — it's what gives chords like the Major 6/9 (Item #1) or the
> Minor 7(9) (Item #2) their warmth. You'll see extensions written in parentheses after the chord name, like
> Cm7(9) — that just means "add the 9th on top."
>
> Guitar chords aren't usually played as a plain stack of those four (or five) notes in order — the notes
> get rearranged across the strings for a shape that's actually playable. This catalog groups those
> rearrangements into a few families you'll see named throughout: a **Shell** voicing keeps just the root,
> 3rd, and 7th (plus the extension), for a lean, open sound. A **Drop 2** or **Drop 3** voicing takes a
> normal stacked chord and drops one note down an octave into the bass, spreading the chord across more
> strings for a fuller sound. A few chords in this set use **custom** voicings that don't fit either
> pattern — built by ear, for a specific color.
>
> Two kinds of notation carry the practice patterns and song excerpts throughout this book.
>
> **TAB** shows you exactly where to put your fingers. The six horizontal lines are the six strings, and a
> number on a line is the fret to play on that string — stacked numbers are a chord, played together. The
> chord name printed above each bar tells you which shape you're in (matching the diagrams elsewhere in this
> book), and the rhythm — how long to hold each note, where the rests fall — is notated the normal way, with
> stems, beams, and rest signs.
>
> **Rhythm pattern grids** show you how to strum or pick, independent of which chord you're playing. Read
> left to right: the top row numbers the beats, and the row (or two rows) underneath show where to strike.
> If a pattern splits the picking hand into thumb and fingers, you'll see them as two separate rows — thumb
> below, fingers above. A tall block means strike there; a darker block is an accent (play it harder), a
> lighter one is a softer hit, and a short flat mark means rest — silence still gets its own beat in the
> grid, it's just drawn low instead of tall.
>
> With the theory out of the way — here are ten of the most useful, recognizable chords in the Bossa Nova
> vocabulary.

## Item #1 — The Major 6/9 Chord

**Citation:** Stan Getz & João Gilberto, *Getz/Gilberto*, 1964 (opening chord of *The Girl from Ipanema*)

**Catalog display root: Db** — shown as **Db6/9/Ab** (5th in the bass), the real performance key, matching
the website's dedicated page. Confirmed per your call — no teaching-key substitution for this one.

### Body copy (adapted from website, length-fit for a catalog item)

> This is *the* chord — the opening voicing of *The Girl from Ipanema* on the Getz/Gilberto recording, and
> the sound that introduced Bossa Nova to the world. Despite its complex name (Major 6/9 with the 5th in
> the bass — João Gilberto's own preference), it's a relaxed, open shape that sits naturally under the
> fingers.

### Voicing note (new section, sits below/beside the diagram — no slugs, plain playing-language)

> This Major 6/9 is a custom voicing rather than a standard chord shape: instead of the usual stack of
> 3rds, the notes here are arranged in 4ths. That's what gives the chord its open, slightly suspended
> sound compared to a conventional Major7 chord.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram — contextual/historical, separate from the voicing note above)

> Interval structure (low to high): 5 · 3 · 6 · 9

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: DB leadsheet `top10`, bars 0–3, transposed up a semitone to match the Db catalog key (same
"shape moved a 4th higher" relationship as the original C→F pattern).

| Bar | Chord | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1–2 | Db6(9)/Ab | home position — matches the song's real key |
| 3–4 | Gb6(9)/Db | same shape, moved up a 4th — the "moveable shape" teaching point |

**Rhythm: Gilberto Rhythm · 2/4 · sixteenth-note grid** (no bpm shown on the grid itself, per your note).
Short text under the grid:

> João Gilberto's signature rhythm — a steady bass pulse from the thumb under off-beat, syncopated chord
> plucks from the fingers, distilled from the more complex patterns of samba.

Two-step practice design (per the existing item #1 prototype, carries forward unchanged):
1. Rhythm grid with count words (`1 e + a 2 e + a` for the 2/4 sixteenth grid).
2. TAB applying that rhythm to the actual chord shape — full chord on "1", chord-only on "+", bass-only on
   "2", chord-only on "e" of beat 2.

### Margin asides

- **Listen:** Stan Getz & João Gilberto, *Getz/Gilberto* (1964).
- **Try this:** This voicing is open enough to sit under almost any two-chord shuttle — experiment freely with different two-chord pairings. Jobim's own favorite: slide the whole shape up a single half-step and back, Db6/9 to D6/9 — that semitone move is a signature of his harmonic language.
- **Related:** The Ellington Progression — Jobim borrowed this chord movement from Duke Ellington's *Take the A-Train* for the opening of *The Girl from Ipanema*; Gilberto's voicing gives it its distinctive Bossa Nova flavor.

## Item #2 — Minor Seventh with 9

**Citation:** Kenny Dorham, *Page One*, 1963 (the A section of *Blue Bossa*)

**Catalog display root: C** — shown as **Cm7(9)** — matches the practice pattern exactly, no key
adjustment needed here.

### Body copy (adapted from website)

> The little sister of the Major 6/9 — a bit more mellow, just as beautiful. The minor 7th with a 9th on
> top softens the chord's dark quality and gives it an airy, floating feel that's essential for minor-key
> Bossa Nova. Kenny Dorham's *Blue Bossa* is built almost entirely around this sound.

### Voicing note

> This is a Shell voicing rooted on the A-string — root, 3rd, and 7th, with the 9th on top. Dropping the
> 5th makes room for that 9th without adding a fifth string — the 9th carries more color here than the 5th
> would. The same shape also works with the 5th in the bass instead of the root, as shown in the example
> below.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> Interval structure (low to high): R · b3 · b7 · 9

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: DB leadsheet `top10`, bars 4–7. No key adjustment needed — already in C, matching the catalog root.

| Bar | Chord | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1–2 | Cm7(9) | home position |
| 3–4 | Fm7(9) | same shape, moved up a 4th |

**Rhythm: Gilberto Rhythm · 2/4 · sixteenth-note grid** (same default as item #1 — this leadsheet carries
no rhythm override of its own).

> João Gilberto's signature rhythm — a steady bass pulse from the thumb under off-beat, syncopated chord
> plucks from the fingers, distilled from the more complex patterns of samba.

Two-step practice design, same pattern as item #1: rhythm grid with count words, then TAB applying that
rhythm to this chord shape.

### Margin asides

- **Listen:** Kenny Dorham, *Page One* (1963).
- **Try this:** Use this chord as the tonic in a minor ii-V-i: Dm7b5 → G7(b9) → Cm7(9). Two chords you already know from elsewhere in this set (the Half Diminished and Dominant b9 voicings) resolving straight into this one — the most common minor cadence in jazz and Bossa Nova alike.
- **Related:** The Minor Jazz Cadence — the progression *Blue Bossa* keeps returning to; the 9th on top is what makes the resolution feel warm rather than heavy.

## Item #3 — The Minor Sixth Chord

**Citation:** João Gilberto, *O Amor, O Sorriso E A Flor*, 1960 (opening chord of *Corcovado*)

**Catalog display root: A** — shown as **Am6**, matches the website exactly.

### Body copy (from website)

> The minor 6th chord replaces the 7th with a major 6th — a subtle swap that changes the mood entirely.
> Where the m7 feels unresolved and floating, the m6 feels grounded and final. That quality of
> stillness-within-minor is the defining color of Jobim ballads like *Corcovado* and *Insensatez*.

### Voicing note

> This is a Drop 3 voicing — one of the four chord tones is dropped down an octave into the bass while
> the rest stack on top. Compared to a Shell voicing, it spreads the chord across more strings, trading
> one extra finger for a fuller, more resonant sound.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> Interval structure (low to high): R · 6 · b3 · 5

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: DB leadsheet `top10`, bars 9–12 (Am6 → Dm7(9)). This also matches the old PDF's own suggested
pattern for this item ("Am – Dm()"), so it's confirmed from both sources.

| Bar | Chord | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1–2 | Am6 | home position — the tonic |
| 3–4 | Dm7(9) | same shape, moved up a 4th |

**Rhythm: Extended Gilberto Rhythm · 2/4 · sixteenth-note grid** (no bpm shown, per your note).

> A variation of the basic Gilberto Rhythm — pulling the first-beat accent a sixteenth note earlier
> gives it more forward drive and momentum than its plainer sister.

### Margin asides

- **Listen:** João Gilberto, *O Amor, O Sorriso E A Flor* (1960).
- **Try this:** Substitute the Minor6 chord for the tonic minor chord in any song.
- **Related:** The Corcovado Progression — pairing this chord with a diminished passing chord; the contrast between settled and restless gives the song its bittersweet, quietly melancholic character.

## Item #4 — Dominant Seventh with 13

**Citation:** Antonio Carlos Jobim, *Wave*, 1967 (the intro's ii–V)

**Catalog display root: G** — shown as **G7(13)**, matches the website exactly.

### Body copy (from website)

> A complicated name for a simple, elegant sound. The 13th adds brightness and color without extra
> tension. It's the dominant chord Jobim used for smooth, sun-drenched resolutions in *Wave*, *The Girl
> from Ipanema*, and dozens of other songs.

### Voicing note

> Another Drop 3 voicing, built the same way as the Minor Sixth shape in Item #3 — one chord tone drops
> out of the usual stack (here the 5th, left out entirely) and the rest spread across four strings with
> two left muted in between. The 13th lands as the highest note in the shape — keep the touch light so
> it sings above the rest.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> Interval structure (low to high): R · b7 · 3 · 13

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: DB leadsheet `top10`, bars 20–21 (Dm7(9) → G7(13)) — the ii–V into this chord, not a 4th-shift
drill this time. This matches both the website's named **Jazz Half Cadence** progression (seed key C:
"G7(13) resolving to CMaj7 is one of the most characteristic sounds in Bossa Nova") and the real *Wave*
intro itself, bars 0–1, which is the exact same Dm7(9) → G7(13) pair.

| Bar | Chord | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Dm7(9) | the ii — sets up the cadence |
| 2 | G7(13) | home position — resolves outward to CMaj7 |

**Rhythm: Jazz Bossa Nova · 4/4 · eighth-note grid** (no bpm shown, per your note).

> The #1 go-to rhythm for giving any bossa song a jazz treatment — use it anywhere.

### Margin asides

- **Listen:** Antonio Carlos Jobim, *Wave* (1967).
- **Try this:** Resolve the cadence all the way: Dm7(9) → G7(13) → Cmaj7. Then move the whole three-chord cell around the cycle of fourths — Gm7(9) → C7(13) → Fmaj7, and on.
- **Related:** The Jazz Half Cadence — one of the most characteristic sounds in Bossa Nova; the 13th creates a warm, optimistic tension that melts cleanly into the major tonic. The cadence at the heart of *Wave*.

## Item #5 — The Half Diminished Chord

**Citation:** Luiz Bonfá, *Solo In Rio*, 1959 (the central ii–V of *Manhã de Carnaval*)

**Catalog display root: B** — shown as **Bm7b5**, matches the website exactly.

### Body copy (from website)

> The half-diminished — also written m7♭5 or ø — is the II chord in every minor jazz cadence. It has an
> unsettled, questioning quality that makes the dominant that follows feel inevitable. Learn this shape
> once and you'll recognize it in *Manhã de Carnaval*, *Chega de Saudade*, *Blue Bossa*, and virtually
> every minor-key standard in the repertoire.

### Voicing note

> This is a Drop 2 voicing rooted on the A-string — one chord tone is dropped down an octave from a
> Shell-style stack, spreading the chord across four strings for a fuller sound than a Shell voicing
> alone.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> Interval structure (low to high): R · b5 · b7 · b3

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: DB leadsheet `top10`, bars 15–16 (Bm7b5 → E7, resolving to Am6) — the minor ii–V–i in its
purest form, matching the website's named **Minor Jazz Cadence** progression exactly ("leans forward
into the dominant and won't feel resolved until it reaches the Am tonic"). The landing chord is this
catalog's own Item #3 tonic.

| Bar | Chord | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Bm7b5 → E7 | the ii–V — unsettled, leaning forward |
| 2 | Am6 | the i — resolves into Item #3's tonic |

**Rhythm: Choro · 2/4 · sixteenth-note grid** (DB slug `bonfa` — no bpm shown, per your note).

> Luiz Bonfá's syncopated guitar comping, in the classic Brazilian Choro style — this exact pattern is
> from his most famous composition, *Manhã de Carnaval*.

### Margin asides

- **Listen:** Luiz Bonfá, *Solo In Rio* (1959).
- **Try this:** Move the whole ii–V–i around the cycle of fourths — Em7b5 → A7 → Dm, then Am7b5 → D7 → Gm, and on. Same suspended-to-resolved feeling, any minor key. Advanced: this same shape is a true chameleon — the identical notes also spell G7(9)/B or Dm(6)/B. Depending on the bass note underneath, one fingering can function as a half-diminished ii, a rootless dominant 9, or a minor 6 tonic.
- **Related:** The Minor Jazz Cadence in its purest form — the half-diminished creates a sense of suspension that won't feel resolved until it reaches the minor tonic.

## Item #6 — Dominant Seventh with 9

**Citation:** Astrud Gilberto, *Fotografia*, 1965 (the chord that closes the intro phrase)

**Catalog display root: D** — shown as **D7(9)**, matches the website exactly.

### Body copy (from website)

> The Dom7(9) shell voicing is one of the most practical chords in the guitar repertoire. The 9th adds
> warmth without complexity, and the open shell structure leaves space for a bassline in the thumb.
> Listen to Astrud Gilberto's recording of Jobim's *Fotografia*: the intro builds a hypnotic cross
> rhythm, and the D7(9) arrives at the end of the phrase like a question mark — tense, bright, and
> perfectly placed.

### Voicing note

> This is a Shell voicing rooted on the A-string — just the root, 3rd, and 7th, with the 9th stacked on
> top for color. Same shape family as Item #2's Cm7(9), just dominant quality instead of minor.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> Interval structure (low to high): R · 3 · b7 · 9

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: the *Fotografia* leadsheet itself, bars 0–3 — the song's actual intro, and a cleaner
illustration of the **Modal Interchange I to Im** progression than anything in the generic `top10`
sequence: I major → Im (borrowed minor) → the D7(9) pivot, which in the full intro resolves back to
Amaj7 right after.

| Bar | Chord | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1–2 | Amaj7 | the major tonic (I) |
| 3 | Am7 | borrowed from the parallel minor (Im) |
| 4 | D7(9) | home position — the bright, modal-interchange dominant, resolving back to Amaj7 just after |

**Rhythm: Cross Rhythm · 4/4 · sixteenth-note grid** (DB slug `cross-rhythm-i` — no bpm shown).

> A syncopated push-pull between thumb and fingers — Astrud Gilberto's *Fotografia* intro is built on
> this exact pattern.

### Margin asides

- **Listen:** *Fotografia* — this exact 4-bar intro; the D7(9) lands right at the end of the phrase.
- **Try this:** Use only this voicing to play a full 12-bar blues — same Dom7(9) shape, just sliding it to the I, IV, and V chords. One shape, three positions, an entire blues.
- **Related:** Modal Interchange — this chord is borrowed from the parallel key, creating a bright, unexpected tension that resolves back to the major tonic.

## Item #7 — Dominant Seventh with b9

**Citation:** Baden Powell, *Solitude on Guitar*, 1973 (the minor ii–V–i into Em, bars 7–10 of *The Shadow of Your Smile*)

**Catalog display root: E** — shown as **E7(b9)**, matches the website exactly.

### Body copy (from website)

> The V7(♭9) is the most tension-laden chord in minor jazz harmony — the ♭9 sits just a half-step above
> the root, creating a dissonance that aches to resolve. Baden Powell leans into that tension in *The
> Shadow of Your Smile* (bars 7–10), voicing the chord as B7(♭9) with its 5th in the bass instead of the
> root, resolving the classic minor ii–V–i straight into Em.

### Voicing note

> Same Shell-voicing family as Item #6's D7(9) — just with a ♭9 instead of a 9 on top, the half-step
> dissonance that makes this the tensest chord in the catalog. The example below shows another way to
> read this same color: Powell voices it with the 5th (F#) in the bass instead of the root, the same
> bass-substitution trick as Item #1's Major 6/9 — and since this voicing has the 5th instead of the
> root, we can also read it as a dim7 chord: F#dim7, the same symmetrical shape as Item #8.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> Interval structure (low to high): R · 3 · b7 · b9

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: DB leadsheet `the-shadow-of-your-smile`, bars 7–10 — a genuine minor ii–V–i in the song's home
key of Em, lifted directly from Baden Powell's arrangement.

| Bar | Chord | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 7 | F#m7 | the ii |
| 8 | B → B7(♭9)/F# | the V — walks up to the ♭9 voicing, an F#dim7 shape with the 5th in the bass |
| 9–10 | Em | the i — resolves home |

Notice the single voice tying it together: the 5th of F#m7 (C#) steps down to the ♭9 of B7(♭9) (C), then
down again to the 5th of Em (B) — a smooth chromatic thread running underneath the changes.

**Rhythm: Basic Picking Pattern · 2/4** (slug `basic-picking-pattern`).

> A plain fingerstyle pattern, no syncopation — letting the chromatic voice-leading carry the phrase on
> its own.

### Margin asides

- **Listen:** Baden Powell, *Solitude on Guitar* (1973).
- **Try this:** Practice this same minor ii–V–i thread — 5th of the iim7 stepping down to the ♭9 of the V7(♭9), then down again to the 5th of the im — in two or three other minor keys. The half-step voice leading is what makes it sound inevitable, not the specific key.
- **Related:** The Minor Jazz Cadence — the same ii–V–i family as Items #2 and #5, here with the ♭9 built as a borrowed diminished shape and the 5th in the bass instead of the root.

## Item #8 — The Diminished Seventh Chord

**Citation:** Stan Getz & João Gilberto, *Getz/Gilberto*, 1964 (the passing diminished in *Desafinado*, bars 36–39)

**Catalog display root: C#** — shown as **C#dim7**, matches the website exactly.

### Body copy (from website)

> Jobim's secret weapon. Every note in a diminished seventh chord can function as the root, meaning one
> shape gives you four chords at once. Jobim exploited this in *How Insensitive*, *Desafinado*, and
> especially *Once I Loved*, where diminished passing chords appear throughout — sliding the shape up or
> down in half-steps to create smooth chromatic motion between any two chords.

### Voicing note

> A Drop 2 voicing of the diminished 7th. Like Items #2, #5, #6, and #7, this voicing sits tightly on the
> middle four strings, so it connects smoothly with any of those shapes without a big jump across the
> neck.

### Voicing caption (under the diagram)

> Interval structure (low to high): R · b3 · b5 · bb7

### Suggested Practice Pattern

Source: DB leadsheet `top10`, bars 18–20 (Cmaj7 → C#dim7 → Dm7(9)) — matches the website's named **The
Ascending Diminished** progression exactly: the °7 acts as a chromatic bridge between the I and the
II7, the same motion Jobim used in *Once I Loved* and the verse of *Desafinado*. This also overlaps
directly with Item #4's bars (Dm7(9) → G7(13) follows right after) — the leadsheet runs these three
items as one continuous phrase.

| Bar | Chord | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cmaj7 | the I |
| 2 | C#dim7 | home position — chromatic bridge, half-step up from C |
| 3 | Dm7(9) | the II7 it resolves into |

**Rhythm: Gilberto Rhythm · 2/4 · sixteenth-note grid** (no per-item override found yet — flag if you
want something else here).

> João Gilberto's signature rhythm — a steady bass pulse from the thumb under off-beat, syncopated chord
> plucks from the fingers, distilled from the more complex patterns of samba.

### Margin asides

- **Listen:** Stan Getz & João Gilberto, *Getz/Gilberto* (1964).
- **Try this:** Because of the symmetry, this one shape already gives you four diminished chords — C#°7, E°7, G°7, B♭°7. Try resolving each a half-step up into a different major or minor chord, and hear four completely different passing-chord contexts from one fingering.
- **Related:** The Ascending Diminished — this chord acts as a chromatic bridge between two chords a half-step apart; the smooth, inevitable motion Jobim used throughout *Once I Loved* and the verse of *Desafinado*.

> enough to feel more impressionistic. This was a characteristic touch of João Gilberto, who used it in

> A Drop 3 voicing of the diminished 7th — symmetrical in the same way as Item #8's diminished, though

> sitting directly above the chord, harmonized straight in. Direct cousin of Item #7's ♭9 dominant: both
> bolt one extra note onto the same diminished-7 logic, just in a different place — a bass note

### Example #1 — The Girl from Ipanema

**Repertoire Example · 1 of 7**

**Bars 5–12 · Stan Getz & João Gilberto, *Getz/Gilberto*, 1964**

**Chords used:** Item #1 · Item #6 · Item #2 · Item #9

`[NOTATION: the-girl-from-ipanema-1, bars 5–12]`

This phrase is built almost entirely from TOP10 chords, just in disguise. The opening Db6(9)/Ab is Item #1
itself — the chord this whole catalog entry is named for. Two bars in, Jobim makes his favorite leap:
straight from the I chord to a II7, the same trick Duke Ellington used in *Take the A-Train* (it's even
called the Ellington Progression). That II7 is Eb7(9)/Bb — Item #6, with its 5th instead of its root in
the bass. Right behind it, Ebm7(9)/Bb does the same disguise to Item #2. Then the cleverest move: the
Ab7(b9,13) chord is a dim7 shape playing dominant — the same family of trick Item #9 teaches, just dressed
differently. The phrase closes with the half-step slide named in Item #1's own practice tip: Db6/9 sliding
up to D6/9 and back.

### Example #2 — So Danço Samba

**Repertoire Example · 2 of 7**

**Bars 0–7 · Stan Getz & João Gilberto, *Getz/Gilberto*, 1964** (this excerpt uses a simplified 8-bar
reduction of the chart, not the full tune)

**Chords used:** Item #1 · Item #6 · Item #10

`[NOTATION: so-danco-samba-jazz, bars 0–7]`

Só Danço Samba (I Only Dance Samba) is a joyful bossa nova classic composed by Tom Jobim with lyrics 
by Vinicius de Moraes in 1962. The most famous recording features João Gilberto on guitar and vocals
from the legendary 1964 album "Getz/Gilberto". This song showcases the American jazz influence on 
bossa nova - the chord progression follows the classic AABA form used in Tin Pan Alley standards.

Jobim runs the same trick as *The Girl from Ipanema* here, just a semitone higher — D major instead of
Db major. The tonic D6/9 is Item #1's family, and two bars in, the same Ellington Progression leap shows
up again: E9, Item #6's quality on a new root. The phrase closes on A7(b13) sliding home to the D6/9
tonic — Item #10's exact quality, and the same guide-tone motion as the catalog: the ♭13 (F) resolves
down a half-step into the 9th of the tonic chord (E).
