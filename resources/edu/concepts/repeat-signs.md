A lead sheet compresses a full arrangement into a single page. Repeat signs, volta brackets, and navigation markers make this possible by encoding the performance structure in shorthand.

<sbn-widget slug="repeat-signs" />

**Repeat barlines** are the most common. A double barline with two dots says: play this section again from the matching repeat sign (or from the beginning if there isn't one). They're everywhere in AABA and verse-chorus forms.

**Volta brackets** (the "1." and "2." boxes above the staff) handle different endings. Play the first ending the first time through; skip it on the repeat and jump straight to the second ending. This avoids copying out the whole section just to change the last bar.

**D.S. al Coda** ("Dal Segno — to the Coda") sends you back to the 𝄋 sign, then redirects to the Coda section when you hit 𝄌 again. **D.C. al Fine** ("Da Capo — from the top") takes you back to the beginning and ends at the word *Fine*.

Jazz lead sheets use all of these. Knowing them fluently means you spend rehearsal time playing, not decoding the road map.
