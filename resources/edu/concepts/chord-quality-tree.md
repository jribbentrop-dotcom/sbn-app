Every common 7th chord grows from one of two triads. The **major triad** is the seed for two qualities; the **minor triad** is the seed for three. Add a single note on top — and occasionally lower the 5th — and you arrive at each chord's character.

**From the major triad:**

- **maj7** — add a major 7th. The triad stays bright; the 7th resolves upward into the octave.
- **dom7** — add a minor 7th instead. The major 3rd stays, but the ♭7 creates a tritone with it — restless, wanting to move.

**From the minor triad:**

- **m7** — add a minor 7th. Both colour tones are lowered; soft and neutral, the ii chord.
- **m7♭5** — flatten the 5th first, then add a minor 7th. The chord loses its footing — the half-diminished sound.
- **dim7** — flatten the 5th *and* lower the 7th to a diminished 7th. All four intervals are stacked minor thirds.

The pattern is a spectrum: each step lowers one more tone, shifting the chord from bright and stable toward dark and tense.
