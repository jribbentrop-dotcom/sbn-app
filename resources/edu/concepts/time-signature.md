The two numbers stacked at the beginning of a piece are the **time signature**. The top number says how many beats are in each bar. The bottom number says what kind of note gets one beat.

<sbn-widget slug="time-signature" />

In **4/4** — the most common metre — there are four quarter-note beats per bar. In **3/4** there are three, giving the circular, lilting feel of a waltz. **6/8** groups six eighth notes into two felt beats of three, producing the compound rolling pulse common in Afro-Cuban music and some Bossa Nova ballads.

Asymmetric metres like **5/4** and **7/8** don't resolve evenly into two or three, which gives them a restless, slightly off-balance quality. Dave Brubeck's *Take Five* made 5/4 famous; 7/8 appears in Balkan-influenced and progressive jazz writing.

**For Bossa Nova and Jazz:** the vast majority of repertoire is in 4/4. Understanding 3/4 is essential for ballads and waltz-feel pieces. 6/8 appears occasionally when the triplet subdivision is made explicit in the rhythm pattern.
