Before barre chords, drop voicings, or jazz extensions, there are eight chords. These open-position shapes are the foundation of guitar playing in virtually every style — folk, rock, pop, classical, and the rhythm patterns that underpin Bossa Nova.

<sbn-widget slug="basic-chords" />

The eight divide neatly: four major (E, A, D, G, C) and four minor (Em, Am, Dm — and G minor is rare in open position, so C stands in as the fifth major). Each chord uses the open strings as part of its voicing, which gives them a resonance that barre chords can't fully replicate.

Two things worth noticing across these shapes:

- **Major vs minor is often a one-finger difference.** Compare E to Em — same two-finger grip, one finger added. Compare A to Am — the triangle moves one string. The minor 3rd is one semitone below the major 3rd; on the fretboard that's one fret.
- **The CAGED shapes are these chords.** Every open shape becomes a movable barre chord when slid up the neck. The E shape barred at fret 2 is F♯ major. The A shape barred at fret 2 is B major. Learning the open shapes is learning the CAGED system.
