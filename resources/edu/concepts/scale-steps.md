Every scale is defined by its pattern of **whole steps** (two semitones) and **half steps** (one semitone). The brackets show the distance between each pair of adjacent notes — taller means a bigger step.

<sbn-widget slug="scale-steps" />

**C major** follows the pattern W–W–H–W–W–W–H. The two half steps land between the 3rd and 4th degrees (E–F) and the 7th and 8th (B–C) — both natural semitones on the piano keyboard.

**C natural minor** rearranges the same ingredients into W–H–W–W–H–W–W. The half steps shift to the 2nd–3rd (D–E♭) and 5th–6th (G–A♭), which is what gives the minor scale its characteristic sound. Three notes are flattened compared to major: the 3rd, 6th, and 7th.

Internalising these patterns — not just for C but across all 12 keys — is the foundation of reading key signatures and understanding why chords sound the way they do.
