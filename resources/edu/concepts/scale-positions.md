A single scale has many homes on the guitar. The **minor pentatonic** — five notes per octave — sits in five overlapping box shapes across the neck. Together they cover every fret, every string, every octave.

<sbn-widget slug="scale-positions" root="A" start-position="1" />

The five positions share the same five notes (in A minor pentatonic: A, C, D, E, G). What changes is *where on the neck* you anchor your hand. Position 1 sits at the 5th fret with the root on the low E string — the classic blues home base. Position 5 is the same pattern an octave higher, ready to loop back to Position 1.

Two things make this useful in practice:

- **Solo geography.** Once you can move between positions without thinking, the entire neck becomes one playground instead of five disconnected boxes.
- **Chord-to-scale links.** Each position lives next to a CAGED chord shape, so you can melt between rhythm and lead without shifting hand position.

The same five shapes also generate **major pentatonic** — just start the pattern three frets lower. C major pentatonic is the same fingering as A minor pentatonic, but the *root* now lives on different notes inside the box.
