A triplet divides a beat into three equal parts instead of two. The "3" written above or below the group is the signal.

<sbn-widget slug="triplets" />

The **eighth-note triplet** is the most important in Jazz and Bossa Nova. Three eighth notes squeezed into one beat. This subdivision is everywhere — even when it isn't written. **Swing feel** is an approximation of the eighth-note triplet: the long-short pattern you hear in Jazz is the first and third notes of a triplet, with the middle note omitted. Knowing the triplet subdivision makes swing feel legible rather than mysterious.

The **quarter-note triplet** spans two beats — three quarter notes in the space of two. It's less common in everyday playing but appears in ballads and as a displacement device when a soloist wants to temporarily pull against the pulse.

A useful check: if you can sing or tap "tri-pl-et, tri-pl-et" evenly over a beat, you're subdividing in triplets. That internal clock is what separates players who approximate swing from those who genuinely feel it.
