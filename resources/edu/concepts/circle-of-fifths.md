The **circle of fifths** arranges all 12 major keys around a clock face. Each
step clockwise is a perfect fifth up (or a perfect fourth down). Every key
sits next to its closest harmonic neighbours.

Click any segment to select a key. The inner ring shows its **relative minor**
— the minor key that shares the same key signature.

**Why fifths?** Moving by a fifth changes only one note from the previous key
signature. C major has no sharps or flats; G major (one step clockwise) adds F♯;
D major adds C♯. Each clockwise step adds a sharp; each anticlockwise step adds a
flat. This is why keys nearby on the circle sound closely related — they share most
of their notes.

Adjacent keys on the circle are the natural choices for **modulation** (moving
from one key to another mid-piece), and they produce the most consonant chord
progressions when combined.
