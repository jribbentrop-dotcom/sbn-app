A **pentatonic scale** strips the seven-note major or natural minor scale down to five notes by removing the two that create half-step clashes. What's left has no friction — every note sits comfortably over the underlying chord.

The **major pentatonic** removes the 4th and 7th. The **minor pentatonic** removes the 2nd and 6th. Both are maximally consonant for their mode — which is why they work so reliably as improvisation tools across blues, rock, and jazz.

In a Jazz or Bossa Nova context, pentatonics serve a different purpose than pure improvisation. They're a tool for **outlining chord tones**: the major pentatonic built on the 5th degree outlines a dominant 7th chord; the one built on the 2nd degree outlines a minor 7th. Advanced players choose which pentatonic to apply based on the chord's function, not just its root — a technique that produces melodic lines that sound deeply inside the harmony.
