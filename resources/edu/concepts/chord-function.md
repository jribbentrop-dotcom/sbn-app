In tonal harmony, chords don't just have a sound — they have a **job**. Every chord in a key belongs to one of three functional families:

- **Tonic** — home. Stable and resolved. Music can rest here.
- **Subdominant** — away from home, but not pulling hard. Creates motion without strong tension.
- **Dominant** — maximum tension. The chord that most wants to return to Tonic.

The arrows in Bossa Nova and Jazz harmony almost always follow the same path: **Subdominant → Dominant → Tonic**. The ii–V–I is the defining example — Dm7 (Subdominant) pulls to G7 (Dominant), which resolves to Cmaj7 (Tonic).

Knowing a chord's function tells you what it wants to do next. It also explains substitutions: two chords in the same family can often swap places, because they share the same harmonic role. The VIm (relative minor) stands in for the I; the bVII stands in for the V. Function is why those swaps work.
