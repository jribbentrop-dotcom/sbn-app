The colour of a chord — bright, tense, dark — is shaped almost entirely by which thirds sit inside it. Start with a **major 7th** and lower one tone at a time: you travel from the sunniest sound in common harmony down to the most shadowy.

The five steps form a continuous spectrum:

- **Cmaj7** — major 3rd + major 7th. Both colour tones are high. Open, resolved, the home sound.
- **C7** — drop the 7th a half-step. The ♭7 introduces restless tension while the major 3rd keeps things bright. This is the dominant chord — it always wants to move.
- **Cm7** — drop the 3rd too. Now both colour tones are minor. Soft and neutral, neither sad nor joyful. The ii chord in every ii–V–I.
- **Cm7♭5** — flatten the 5th as well. The chord loses its footing: unstable and shadowy, the half-diminished sound that opens minor ii–V–I progressions.
- **C°7** — lower the 7th once more to a diminished 7th. All four intervals are now stacked minor thirds. Fully symmetrical, rootless-feeling, the darkest and most ambiguous quality of all.

The same logic applies to any root. Recognising which tones have been lowered — and by how much — is what lets you read a chord symbol at a glance and hear its emotional weight before a note is played.
